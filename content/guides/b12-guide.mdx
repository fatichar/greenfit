## Introduction

Vitamin B12 keeps nerves, blood cells, and DNA production working properly. It is also the plant-based nutrient where guessing is least helpful: unfortified plant foods are not reliable sources, and deficiency can become serious before it feels obvious.

The good news is that B12 is easy to solve. Use fortified foods if they are consistent in your diet, use a supplement if they are not, and confirm with blood work when you are unsure. For a supplement overview, open the [Vitamin B12 page](/supplements/vitamin-b12).

## Acceptable range and blood testing

B12 is one of the guides where testing is genuinely useful. A serum B12 result is commonly reported in **pg/mL** or **pmol/L**, but the "normal" range can vary by lab.

- **Serum B12:** Many labs flag deficiency below roughly **200 pg/mL**, but levels between **200 and 300 pg/mL** can still be borderline.
- **Practical target:** Many clinicians prefer seeing B12 comfortably above the borderline zone, often **300 pg/mL or higher**.
- **MMA and homocysteine:** If serum B12 is borderline or symptoms do not match the number, **methylmalonic acid (MMA)** and **homocysteine** can help identify functional deficiency.

If you have numbness, tingling, balance changes, unexplained anemia, or neurological symptoms, do not wait for a diet tweak to work. Get medical care.

## Reliable sources

For a fully plant-based diet, there are two dependable routes: fortified foods and supplements.

- **Fortified soy milk:** Useful because it can also support protein and calcium. See [Fortified Soy Milk](/products/fortified-soy-milk) and try it in [Creamy Peanut Butter Oats](/recipes/peanut-butter-oats).
- **Fortified nutritional yeast:** Convenient on pasta, popcorn, tofu scramble, dal, or salads, but only if the label lists B12.
- **Fortified cereals and meat alternatives:** Helpful backup sources when labels are clear.
- **Supplements:** The most predictable option, especially for people who do not eat fortified foods daily.

Fortification varies by brand and country. Read the nutrition label instead of assuming every plant milk, cereal, or mock meat contains B12.

## Dosage and maintenance

B12 dosing looks high because absorption per dose is limited. That is normal and expected.

- **Daily maintenance:** **50 to 100 mcg daily** is a common supplement range for healthy adults eating plant-based.
- **Twice weekly maintenance:** **1,000 to 2,000 mcg twice weekly** is another common pattern.
- **Fortified-food approach:** If relying on food, include fortified foods more than once daily so absorption is spread out.

The [Vitamin B12 page](/supplements/vitamin-b12) shows the kind of form, dose, and label details to review. If you are already deficient, use clinician-guided treatment instead of a maintenance plan.

## Symptoms of deficiency

B12 deficiency can develop slowly because the body stores some B12, but symptoms can become serious.

- Fatigue, weakness, or shortness of breath
- Brain fog, memory trouble, mood changes, or irritability
- Pale or slightly yellow skin
- Pins and needles, numbness, or burning sensations in hands or feet
- Balance issues, clumsiness, or walking changes
- A sore, smooth, or swollen tongue

These symptoms can overlap with iron deficiency, thyroid issues, low calories, sleep debt, and other conditions. If symptoms are significant, test rather than guessing.

## Bioavailability and timing

B12 absorption depends on intrinsic factor, stomach acid, and a small active-absorption pathway. A tiny percentage of large supplement doses is also absorbed passively, which is why weekly or twice-weekly high-dose supplements can work.

- B12 can be taken **with or without food**.
- If high-dose B12 feels stimulating, take it **morning or early afternoon**.
- If using fortified foods, spread them through the day rather than trying to get everything at one meal.

## Myths

**Myth: Unwashed vegetables and soil provide enough B12.**
*Fact:* Any B12 from soil contamination is unreliable and unsafe to rely on. Modern food hygiene is good for public health, but it means B12 needs a deliberate source.

**Myth: Spirulina, kombucha, and fermented foods are reliable B12 sources.**
*Fact:* Some contain inactive B12-like compounds. They should not replace fortified foods or a supplement.

**Myth: If I feel fine, my B12 must be fine.**
*Fact:* Deficiency can be slow and subtle. People eating fully plant-based should build in a reliable source before symptoms appear.
